His Purpose

Mission & Philosophy

Non-violence as the foundation for all human progress

Lokesh Muni's life mission is rooted in one profound conviction: that Ahimsa — non-violence in its deepest sense — is not merely a religious ideal but a universal, practical force capable of transforming individuals, communities, and the world.

Core Pillars

01

Ahimsa — Non-Violence

The bedrock principle. Ahimsa means not merely avoiding physical harm, but cultivating an active love and compassion toward all living beings — in thought, speech, and action. It is the most radical and the most demanding moral commitment a person can make.

02

Interfaith Unity

Every great tradition holds a spark of divine truth. Lokesh Muni believes that the future of humanity depends on our ability to recognize what unites us across religious boundaries — the shared call to compassion, justice, and reverence for life.

03

Peace Diplomacy

Peace is not the absence of conflict — it is an active, ongoing practice. Lokesh Muni engages with heads of state, international organizations, and civil society to build formal structures and sustained relationships that make peace more durable than war.

04

Education & Youth

The transformation of the world begins in the minds of the young. Lokesh Muni invests deeply in education — teaching non-violence not as abstract theory but as a lived skill: conflict resolution, empathy training, and ethical leadership for the next generation.

The Philosophy

Anekantavada — The Many-Sidedness of Truth

Central to Lokesh Muni's worldview is the Jain concept of Anekantavada — the doctrine that truth is complex, multi-faceted, and can only be fully understood by considering multiple perspectives simultaneously.

In an era of polarization and absolutism, this ancient wisdom offers a radical alternative: that my truth and your truth are not necessarily in conflict, but rather partial glimpses of a larger whole. True dialogue, true peace, begins when we stop insisting on the supremacy of our single perspective.

This philosophical commitment shapes everything Lokesh Muni does — from intimate personal conversations to the design of international interfaith forums.

The Vision

A World Where Ahimsa Is Law

Lokesh Muni's ultimate vision is not utopian but practical: a world where non-violence is enshrined not only in personal ethics but in law, policy, and international relations. Where nations resolve disputes through dialogue rather than arms. Where economic systems do not require exploitation. Where every human being — regardless of faith, origin, or identity — is treated as sacred.

This vision is ancient in its roots and utterly contemporary in its urgency. The crises of our time — climate change, political violence, religious extremism, economic inequality — all find their common root in the failure of compassion. Ahimsa is not one solution among many. It is the solution.

"Every religion, in its purest form, is a path to peace. The tragedy of our age is that we have allowed the paths to become walls."

— Lokesh Muni

The Practice of Ahimsa

For Lokesh Muni, Ahimsa is not passive. It is the most demanding form of action — requiring extraordinary courage, wisdom, and sustained commitment. He identifies three dimensions of non-violent practice:

I

Mansa — Non-Violence in Thought

The cultivation of a mind free from hatred, prejudice, and ill-will — not through repression, but through the genuine understanding of the other's humanity. Practices include meditation, compassionate reflection, and the conscious questioning of one's own assumptions.

II

Vacha — Non-Violence in Speech

Words carry immense power to heal or to harm. Truthful, kind, and constructive speech is an essential component of the Ahimsa practice — in personal conversations, public discourse, and media.

III

Kaya — Non-Violence in Action

Refraining from physical harm in all its forms — not only direct violence but also structural and institutional forms of harm, including exploitation, discrimination, and environmental destruction.

"Ahimsa is a seed. Plant it in the soil of your own heart first. Then it will grow and give shade and fruit to all who come near you."

— Lokesh Muni
